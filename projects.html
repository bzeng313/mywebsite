RNN Prediction Model for Tesla Stock Prices with Tweet Sentiment

During sophomore Fall I decided to take CS221: AI Principles and Techniques. It was a pretty interesting class, and as far as introductory AI courses go, I'd say take it if you just want to dip your toes in the water. For the final project, I partnered with my friend Zheng Lian to work on a model for predicting Tesla stock prices based on Twitter sentiment. To do this, we scraped a couple thousand tweets using Chrome's webscraper tool and took the stock data for the 2018 year from Yahoo! Finance. From this data we conducted some sentiment analysis on the tweets, fed our sentiment data and stock data into an RNN with an LSTM layer and... well, you can kind of see by the graphs above that it didn't work out so well. Which was kind of expected.

Our sentiment analysis on the tweets was done via a pretrained model (using Textblob), so any sentiment data we got from that probably wouldn't have been accurate. From our paper: "Textblob was most likely trained on text with sentiment labeled in terms of moods like happy, sad, or neutral. However, the sentiment of a sentence in terms of stock trends must be classified in terms of its effect on the market. For instance, the tweet 'Tesla goes bankrupt' implies a neutral sentiment, as this is a non-opinionated statement. However, the sentiment in terms of Tesla's stock trend should be negative. If more time and resources are available, we would like to label our own data to train a sentiment analysis model that better represents the sentiment and subjectivity elicited in the tweets in terms of stock market movement." But alas, we didn't have much time to make our own "stock sentiment" classifier, so we just went with the simplest tools we had. In the end, our stock predictor didn't really fair that much more well than linear regression, but hey, it was a great learning experience, and I'm glad I learned how to actually implement a neural network.

XY Compliance for Stanford Doggo

As a part of the Stanford Robotics Club's Extreme Mobility team, one thing I worked on was adding virtual xy compliance to our robot, Doggo. Normally, the movement of Doggo's legs would be constrained at specific theta and gamma angles, and the torques exerted by the motors to get Doggo's legs back to those angles were proportional to the leg's displacement. I became interested in adding xy compliance to Doggo's legs, so instead of theta and gamma constraints, I wanted to have Doggo's legs be constrained to specific xy axes.

Turns out by multiplying a leg's xy force vector by the transpose of the Jacobian, you get the theta-gamma torque vector. Using this idea and PD control, we were able to constrain Doggo's legs to move in a single axis. The above gif shows Doggo's legs constrained at x=0.05m. Pretty cool.
